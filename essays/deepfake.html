Deepfake and the Crisis of Digital Trust

Sulthonika Mahfudz Al Mujahidin By Sulthonika Mahfudz Al Mujahidin January 6, 2025

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has made the line between reality and digital engineering increasingly difficult to distinguish. What was once only an experimental technology has now turned into an instrument capable of mimicking human faces, voices and expressions with a precision that is no longer distinguishable by the ordinary eye. In the fast-paced digital atmosphere, deepfake has emerged as a symbol of that change. It's not just an engineered image or sound, but a challenge to the way society understands evidence and authenticity online. Many studies have shown that these technologies erode the foundations of public trust in digital content because people no longer have a clear dividing line between what is created by humans and what is generated by algorithms (Chesney & Citron, 2019). This uncertainty is the beginning of a digital trust crisis.

When people start to doubt what they see, the problem does not stop at the visual realm. Increasingly sophisticated technological options also allow for the manipulation of sound in a much more convincing way than just a few years ago. Studies on the development of voice synthesis models show that modern technology is able to subtly mimic a person's intonation and speech patterns so that listeners do not easily notice the manipulation (Mirsky & Lee, 2021). These changes mean that the deepfake threat extends beyond the debate about fake content. It shifts the issue to a realm closer to everyday life because voice is a medium that is commonly used in a variety of personal and professional interactions. When voice can be faked so convincingly, trust collapses not only on a public level, but also on a personal level.

“Deepfakes not only create new lies, but also undermine society's ability to trust actual evidence.”

The collapse of trust is even more pronounced in social and political contexts. Studies show that manipulative content tends to spread faster than verified content, especially when it touches on sensitive issues such as identity politics or social conflict (Vaccari & Chadwick, 2020). Society then falls into a paradoxical situation. On the one hand, they are increasingly wary of content that looks dramatic. On the other hand, they are also more vulnerable because their overwhelming sense of doubt makes them more likely to believe narratives that fit their personal prejudices. It is at this point that a phenomenon called the liar's dividend emerges. This concept describes the condition when someone who has actually done something wrong can deny the evidence by saying that the content is just a deepfake (Chesney & Citron, 2019). In other words, deepfakes not only create new lies, but also undermine society's ability to trust actual evidence.

This crisis becomes even more apparent when looking at how digital platforms play a role in the spread of manipulative content. Distribution algorithms that prioritize speed and user engagement make it easy for deepfake content to reach a wide audience in a short period of time. Some studies note that the spread of fake content often outperforms true content due to its sensational nature and emotional engagement (West et al., 2021). As the digital ecosystem encourages attention over accuracy, it becomes increasingly difficult for people to find a stable footing to distinguish truth from fabrication.

Despite the seriousness of the threat, it is important to understand that deepfake technology did not grow out of malicious intent. Several studies have shown that the technology was originally developed for legitimate purposes, such as film production, archival restoration, and educational content creation (Agarwal et al., 2019). These positive uses are still relevant today. Many creators utilize the technology to create animations, simulations, or interactive content that would otherwise be costly. In the field of accessibility, deepfake audio is even used to help individuals who have lost the ability to speak through personalized voice reconstruction.

Amidst the tug-of-war between benefits and threats, the inevitable question arises as to how society responds. Although various artificial intelligence studies have developed detection tools to identify manipulative content, researchers agree that no system is absolutely perfect (Aneja et al., 2022). Manipulation patterns are constantly evolving, while detection models take time to adapt to new forms of manipulation. Ultimately, detection technologies can only serve as an initial layer of protection, not as an absolute solution. The role of humans remains necessary to assess context, read patterns of dissemination, and weigh the consistency of information.

Public policy studies confirm the same idea. Efforts to overcome deepfakes cannot rely solely on technology because the issue of trust is far more complex than technical issues (West et al., 2021). People need digital literacy that helps them recognize the signs of manipulation, understand how information circulates, and perform simple verification. On the other hand, digital platforms need to exercise ethical responsibility through stricter moderation and algorithm transparency. Deepfakes may present a major challenge, but society's ability to adapt and develop new vigilance remains a key asset to weathering this crisis.
